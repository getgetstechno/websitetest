Anatomo-physiological reminder

The spine is divided into three movable sections, from top to bottom: the cervical spine (C1 to C7), the thoracic spine (T1 to T12), and the lumbar spine (L1 to L5). At these levels, the vertebrae articulate with each other.

From the side, the spine displays physiological curves that are perfectly balanced with each other. These curves are called "kyphosis" and "lordosis." They alternate anatomically: the cervical spine in lordosis is balanced by the thoracic spine in kyphosis.

The spine contains the spinal cord and the cauda equina nerves, which travel through the spinal canal, the central part of the vertebrae. The spinal cord continues from the brain and directly distributes nerves (nerve roots) at the neck and thoracic levels. The spinal cord ends at the level of the first lumbar vertebra, giving rise to a bundle of roots that supply the perineum and the legs.

An injury to the cervical or thoracic spine directly threatens the spinal cord. The spinal cord is extremely fragile, and the chances of recovery in cases of paralysis are low. An injury at the cervical level can cause tetraplegia (complete paralysis) or tetraparesis (incomplete paralysis). Indeed, a lesion acts like a barrier, causing an interruption of nerve impulses below the injury site.

Pathologies of the spinal cord following trauma

Paraplegia results from damage to the spinal cord and its roots or from significant damage to the cauda equina roots. Strictly speaking, paraplegia means paralysis of the lower limbs, but in practice, it is almost always of spinal origin. Tetraplegia refers to paralysis caused by a cervical injury (tetra meaning four limbs). Together, these types of injuries account for about one-third of spinal cord injuries. Accidents are the leading cause, accounting for 70 to 80% of cases.

How do you know if a victim needs to be immobilised?

The Nexus score (National Emergency X-Radiography Utilization Study) is used to determine whether a patient should undergo a cervical X-ray after a trauma. If the Nexus score is unfavorable, there is a suspicion of a spinal injury. Therefore, immobilization of the spine is required from the scene of the accident until the X-ray room at the hospital.
